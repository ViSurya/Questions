Prove that √3 is a irrational number.

Let us assume that √3 is a rational number. Then, we can express it as a fraction of two integers, p and q, where q ≠ 0 and p and q have no common factors other than 1.

Squaring both sides of the equation √3 = p/q, we get 3 = p2/q2.

This implies that p2 = 3q2, which means that p2 is divisible by 3.

Since p2 is divisible by 3, p must also be divisible by 3 (because if p were not divisible by 3, then p2 would not be divisible by 3).

Let p = 3k for some integer k. Substituting this into the equation p2 = 3q2, we get (3k)2 = 3q2, which simplifies to 9k2 = 3q2.

Dividing both sides of this equation by 3, we get 3k2 = q2. This implies that q2 is divisible by 3, which means that q must also be divisible by 3.

Therefore, both p and q are divisible by 3, which contradicts our assumption that p and q have no common factors other than 1.

Hence, our assumption that √3 is a rational number is false, and we can conclude that √3 is an irrational number.
