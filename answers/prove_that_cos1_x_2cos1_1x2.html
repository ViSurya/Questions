Prove that cos^–1 x = 2cos^–1 √((1+x)/2)

Let \(y = \cos^{-1} x\). Then, \(\cos y = x\). Using the identity \(\cos 2\theta = 1 - 2 \sin^2 \theta\), we have

$$\cos 2y = 1 - 2 \sin^2 y = 1 - 2 (1 - \cos^2 y) = 2 \cos^2 y - 1$$

Substituting \(\cos y = x\) and simplifying, we get

$$\cos 2y = 2x^2 - 1$$

Therefore, \(2y = \cos^{-1} (2x^2 - 1)\), which implies that \(y = \frac{1}{2} \cos^{-1} (2x^2 - 1)\). Substituting \(y = \cos^{-1} x\), we get

$$\cos^{-1} x = \frac{1}{2} \cos^{-1} (2x^2 - 1)$$
